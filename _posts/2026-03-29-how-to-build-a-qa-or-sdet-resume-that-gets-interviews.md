---
title: How to Build a QA or SDET Resume That Gets Interviews
description: A practical guide to making QA and SDET resumes clearer, stronger, and more credible.
---
If you want more interviews for QA or SDET roles, your resume needs to do more than list tools. It needs to make your testing work feel real.

Most resumes lose strength in three places:

- they describe duties instead of outcomes
- they mention tools without explaining how they were used
- they include projects that are hard for recruiters to interpret quickly

## Start with clear role targeting

Make it obvious whether you are aiming for manual QA, automation QA, or SDET roles. If your resume tries to speak to every possible quality role, it usually feels weaker to all of them.

Use your summary and project bullets to reinforce the direction you want:

- QA candidates should emphasize exploratory testing, bug reporting, test case design, and collaboration
- automation QA candidates should show framework usage, regression coverage, and maintainability
- SDET candidates should show deeper technical ownership, tooling decisions, and engineering fluency

## Rewrite bullets around evidence

Weak bullet:

> Performed testing for a web application.

Stronger bullet:

> Designed regression coverage for a web application using Playwright, documented critical bugs with reproducible steps, and improved release confidence before deployment.

The goal is to show what you tested, how you approached it, and why it mattered.

## Treat projects like proof, not homework

If you list a testing project, it should be easy to understand:

- what system you tested
- why you chose the tool or framework
- what scenarios you covered
- what tradeoffs or limitations you handled

Even one clean, readable project can do more for you than several rushed repositories.

## Final resume check

Before you apply, ask:

- does the first screen of the resume show role fit?
- do my bullets explain testing impact?
- do my projects look like evidence of how I think?
